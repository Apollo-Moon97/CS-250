# Journal 

- How do I interpret user needs and implement them into a program? How does creating user stories help with this? -

Usually, I start by trying to understand what the user is actually trying to do, not just what feature they are asking for. That helped me a lot. Looking at the client goals and the focus group feedback helped me see that the point was to build something people would actually want to use. Things like ranked destinations, price filters, and recommendations based on travel history all came from real user wants, not just from what sounded good on paper.

User stories especially helped with that because they forced me to think about the user, the task, and the reason behind it. It put me in their shoes, in a sort of way, and that made the work feel more grounded. It also made it easier to turn bigger ideas into smaller pieces that could actually be developed and tested. Without that, I think it would have been a lot easier to build something that worked correctly but missed what the user actually needed.

- How do I approach developing programs? What Agile processes do I hope to incorporate into my future development work? -

I think I do better when I break things into smaller clearly defined steps. trying to figure everything out all at once usually just makes the work messier, and mentally it even makes it seem insurmountable at times. During this course, it made more sense to move from stories to acceptance criteria to development to testing and then adjust when something changed. That feels a whole lot more realistic to me than pretending the whole project could be fully figured out at the beginning.

Looking forward, I’d like to keep using user stories, acceptance criteria, regular testing, and shorter feedback loops. I also really like the idea of backlog refinement and frequent check ins, even if they are informal, because they help catch some confusion before it turns into a bunch of rework.

- What does it mean to be a good team member in software development? -

Being a good team member means being clear, reliable, and owning your role. It means asking questions early, giving useful updates to others, and importantly, paying attention to how your work affects the rest of the team. This doesn't just apply to software development, but are things I think about everyday in my current job. A project can go sideways pretty fast if people stay quiet when they are confused or just focus on their own part and ignore everything around it. 

Another thing this course helped me see is that every role matters in a different way. The Product Owner keeps the work tied to user value, the Scrum Master keeps the process moving, the developer builds the feature, and the tester makes sure the result actually matches the story. A good team member respects that and works with the team as a unit instead of acting like their part is the only part that matters. These general ideas are things I've begun to think about even in my day to day work already.
